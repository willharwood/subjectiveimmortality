Preface

What this book is about, why I chose to write it (and how).

Why did I write a book on subjective immortality? I wrote it because there are problems I have been thinking about for decades, since I was a child, and books are the way we understand difficult things, by reading them carefully or writing them with even more care. A book, not an article or a predigested summary or a meandering podcast, because understanding requires changing the mind, and a book is a journey that changes the mind.

This is a book of philosophy and I am not a philospher; it is a work of metaphysics and I do not want to be a crank. Why write this book if I am not qualified to write it? Because although cleverer people than me have been thinking and writing about these problems for thousands of years, no one has solved them. Philosphers have brought clarity, albeit clarity for other philosphers. While any book on physics written in the past 100 years by a non-physisist, or in biology for the past sixty years by a non-biologist, is guaranteed to be either shallow or nonsense because of the sheer mass of technical material one must dig through in order to reach the frontier of knowledge and advance an inch further, the problems I am thinking about remain, as it were, on the surface, accessible to anyone willing to reach out their hand. The technical jargon is either clear (the charming Hard and Soft Problems) or can be jettisoned in favour of clearer writing (haecceitism, or thisness?). Most writing on consciousness from the truly qualified is written for the truly qualified. Some things are so difficult to think about that anyone can think about them. One's reach should exceed one's grasp.

Have I proved in this book that subjective immortality is real? No, it is not provable; and also no, in that my conclusion (which I can only nudge an intuition towards, not prove) is an or between two extremes: either there is nothing but subjective immortality, or there is no form of subjective continuity, not even from one moment to the next. Either oblivion does not exist or oblivion is what arrives directly after the current moment. I do believe that the natural view, of a self that lasts from birth until death, the view most of us believe, the view that is so obviously true that it goes undoubted and unexplored, is false. And that there is a comfort to be gained in knowing that it is false. This book is my attempt to understand and justify that claim.

One more note: I have not used generative AI, or AI of any sort, in writing this book. Not in research, not in drafting, not in a single word written here. First, because AI is too easily a shortcut, yet the journey is the point -- one both cheats and is cheated in asking a machine to furnish a plausible text. Second, because I want to find something new, not merely explore the distilled internet. Third, for the selfish reason that I want this to be my own possession. If real Luddism is too difficult, I can cherish a sort of Luddism at home. And finally, because anything worth the time to read is worth many times that time to write. You can see the entire history of this book here: https://github.com/willharwood/subjectiveimmortality/commits/master/book. Every paragraph as it comes together, is edited, rethought, refined, ruined, deleted, then retyped. This is what remains. Technical writing requires trust in a way that other forms of writing do not, because language is an approximation of thought, and it is the thought that one tries, imperfectly, to transmit. The reader has to assume that a real thought is there. If words are a box, then the box is not empty.

Introduction

What is Subjective immortality (and the foolishness of objective immortality). The common-sense view, a materialist position from which we will not deviate. The belief in oblivion, and our first proof that it does not exist, which rests on arguments against the need for strict continuity (of time, of life, of matter). But then the implications of not needing continuity: exactly what is immortal? Towards the Bundled Dichotomy, that experience is either momentary, or eternal; anattā; another, weirder, far more difficult way in we are subjectively immortal.

When Dawn asked Zeus to make her lover Tithonus immortal, she forgot to ask for eternal youth. He aged, "here at the quiet limit of the world", envious "of happy men that have the power to die" in Tennyson's telling, and lives still, withered into a cicada that can barely call and cannot sleep. Immortality is one of those things we all have in common: from the richest to the poorest, it will elude us, and attempts to find it are sad or comical. The first emperor of China sent teams of alchemists and wizards to the far corners of the world in search of elixirs of eternal life, then died of mercury poisoning after consuming the results. Cryonics, the practice of freezing the head of the recently deceased in the hope of reviving them in the distant future, once the cure for having one's head cut off and filled with antifreeze is found, takes wishful thinking into the realms of myth, and adds a barrel of predatory capitalism. What awful promise, at best, is proferred. (Dennis Potter offered a vivid example in his final television play, Cold Lazarus. I watched it thirty years ago and can still bring to mind Albert Finney's head, unconnected to anything human or humane.) Some creatures are said to be biologically immortal, meaning that they do not age, or can see-saw between younger and older forms. That the chances of death do not increase with age is less helpful than one might think, when the chances of death are high to begin with at any age.

How to explain of our current crop of plutocrats the lack of interest in an artistic or cultural legacy? There are no modern Carnegie Libraries, no new Nobel Prize worth the name, the Guggenheim Museum has no competition from any tech billionaire, there is no unselfish principle they wish to outlive them. In Silicon Valley there is only one museum worth visiting, and it is the Computer History Museum. It is not mere philistinism, or not just that. It seems that many believe that they will, personally, live forever; that their legacy will be themselves; and, richer than the emperor of China, they are ploughing funds into making it a reality. We can wait and see how that turns out (or not, given that none of us are immortal). Betting on disappointment has never failed in the history of eucaryotic life. But such objective immortality is not what this book is about. We are interested in a different sort of immortality, not of a single body lurching through time like a Nineteenth Dynasty mummy; instead, an immortality of experience, of feeling, immortality as the opposite of oblivion.

You are currently experiencing reading this sentence, as part of a melange of experience. Perhaps music is playing, there are voices in another room. You are the locus of sensation, the there of something being there. (My current experience: it is the late evening, neither warm nor cold in the Berkeley Hills, not far from Oakland, of which Gertrude Stein once said, "There is no there there". My cat loudly licks one white paw, the only other sound is the shallow click of this keyboard.) One moment, and then the next. We have to believe, when looking at another person, looking back at us, that there too is a unique view on the universe. It is easy to believe this of some animals too, it is not a matter of language. Who could observe an orangutan and not know that the orangutan is observing back? Pets too, with less thought yet no less feeling. It need not be a matter of intelligence per se; or rather, the boundary between conscious and non-conscious might be very far down. It seems likely that a nervous system is a prerequisite, though panpsycist theories would quibble there. For a plant to be conscious would seem a waste. For an ant though, simple and rule-following? To watch an ant pause to clean itself, to really look at an ant and consider its senses, what it sees and smells, is to pause ourselves.

Experience is not a continuous thing, dreamless sleep and other forms of unconsciousness punctuate our days. We skip through time to the next conscious moment. Those who strive for objective immortality believe that without it, these experiences must end. It is a reasonable belief, albeit one that is historically unusual. To picture an eternity of oblivion is like imagining an absense, not a dark corner or black space, a true absense. Oblivion can be named, talked and reasoned about, but not pictured. What is it like not to be? It is a historically unusual belief because much of the brocade of religion exists to deny it. There is this world, and the next; there is something apart from the physical, which has the ability to survive, and moreover, whatever the mirror says, is closer to the true you. For several reasons we will not consider this option. Once you admit the supernatural, what can really be said? And I do not think it is true. I can be clear that this is just as much a belief as any other, based on my partial view and life-so-far and a sincere attempt to keep some rigour. I think, on the balance of probabilities, that the universe is random, what meaning there is is the meaning we try to give it (and we do have to try), that life itself is a sort of miracle of improbability, conscious life doubley so. There is the physical world, of forces and matter, and the fact that certain collections of matter, at certain times, experience the universe is a true mystery, perhaps never to be solved. The biggest fact that there is.

There is another reason why, in this book, we will stick to a physicalist or materialist view, atoms in motion and nothing else. And that is because in being consistent here, we can arrive at some startling conclusions, conclusions that look taken from a very different philosophy. That oblivion, in the sense we have been using it as the infinite stretch of nothing after the last conscious moment is had, does not exist. That there will never be a last conscious moment. That, even as objective immortality is a fool's errand, subjective immortality is real. Is inescapable, in fact.

Let us start at the end, and the real purpose of this book. It is to use the assumed fact of physicalism, and a number of thought experiments, to arrive at the following dichotomy, that exactly one of the following two statements is true:

  1. Subjective experience never ends, there is no such thing as oblivion. This is a sort of immortality.
  2. Subjective experience is a momentary phenomenon. Not only is oblivion real, it is what we experience in the very next moment after this.

Either we are subjectively immortal, or we do not even have subjective continuity from one moment to the next. Experience is forever or for a single moment, with no option in between. Those trying to endlessly extend the lives of their bodies have missed the point in the most profound way one can imagine.

Ways to be immortal

Skipping from Lucretius to Montaigne to Hume, and then back to Lucretius, and the questions about the fear of death he asks. Lucretius is trying to explain the Epicurian view of death ("when I am, death is not; when death is, I am not"). It is a view that believes in oblivion, and here we could make a comparison to Buddhism (if a shallow one). It is the aim of this book to banish the idea of oblivion. We give a first sketch of a proof, asserting that if it could be shown that if whatever it is that is you must, over a long enough timescale, inevitably return, then we can turn Lucretius' questions around, and say that there is simply no end to subjective experience. That is subjective immortality. We start on one avenue to make that argument, Functionalism, though conclude it is a little too easy, that Functionalism itself is a cheat. We then describe Quantum Suicide, an occasionally modeish thought experiment that does double-duty as an empirical way to distinguish the Many Worlds and Copenhagen interpretations of reality, and an argument for subjective immortality. It is cute, and we also find it wanting, as it implicitly contains a sort of dualism. It pictures our conscious selves as some unit connected to yet separate from the body, but we have to reject that view, if we are to keep to physicalism. We end by trying to make this mistake clear.

Three quotations to set the scene

Lucretius wrote De rerum Natura in the first century BC. It is a poetic explication and exploration of Epicurian philosophy, and contains a wonderful argument against the fear of death, of our own personal extinction:

Look back -- think how little you care
For the endless ages of time
That passed, unknown, before your birth.
Nature holds a mirror for you
In which that time is reflected
And welcomes the infinity
That will come after you have died.
Why fret? Where is the tragedy?
Death is a greater peace than sleep.

De rerum natura was lost for over a thousand years, then helped kindle the Renaissance. Michel de Montaigne was deeply influenced by it, and wrote in his copy:

As there are varied movements of atoms, it is not incredible that once upon a time atoms came together, or will come together again, so that another Montaigne is born.

Almost a hundred years later, David Hume wrote in his Treasise of Human Nature:

[W]hen I enter most intimately into what I call myself, I always stumble on some particular perception or other, of heat or cold, light or shade, love or hatred, pain or pleasure. I never can catch myself at any time without a perception, and never can observe any thing but the perception. When my perceptions are removed for any time, as by sound sleep; so long am I insensible of myself, and may truly be said not to exist.

The sceptical, atheistic intellectual tradition of Hume, Montaigne, and Lucretius is alive in the modern scientific, materialist view of the universe. Let's call it the common-sense view, that there is only the physical world. If life is like Bede's sparrow flying through a lighted hall, then nothing exists but that hall. It is common sense because we know that the body does not last forever so why should our consciousness, so manifestly supported by the body, continue to exist after the body has ceased to function? We are exactly our physical bodies, there is no separate mind-stuff, no lurking ghost. We are special arrangements of matter, so special that we can think and feel and somewhat know ourselves, as partial and temporary perspectives on the universe. An endless age of time passed before we existed, and an infinity will pass after we have gone.

Lucretius gives us two questions:

  1. What was it like to not exist in the past?
  2. What will it be like to not exist in the future?

He considers that both questions are the same question and have the same answer:

  1. What was it like to not exist in the past? Oblivion.
  2. What will it be like to not exist in the future? Oblivion.

But consider the first question, and ask: what was it like, for you, a million years ago? Of course it was like nothing. And yet, your subjective experience was a million years immediately passed, and you became conscious for the first time. Consider the second question, which is another way of asking "what is it like to be dead?" Recall Michel de Montaigne's note, of chance forming another de Montaigne (a pun: of atoms eventually becoming a mountain). If we are just our physical beings, then consciousness simply is a certain arrangement of atoms. In a universe that is infinitely wide or lasts infinitely long, every pattern will reappear, and with it (since there is nothing but the pattern) that consciousness will reappear too. You die, and the pattern of your consciousness is lost. Wait long enough, and by pure random motion, whatever arrangement is needed to be you will form, somewhere in the universe. The distance of time or space is immaterial, as nothing is travelling, nothing exists until, of a sudden, it does.

This gives us an answer to the second question, and it is the same answer as to the first:

  1. What was it like to not exist in the past? Immediate subjective experience.
  2. What will it be like to not exist in the future? Immediate subjective experience.

Not existing in the future is the same as not existing in the past. In both cases, what it is like is: you are immediately conscious.

Implicit in the common-sense view is a belief in oblivion. It is impossible to imagine not existing: to imagine a thing is to picture yourself next to it, and yet there is no self to picture anywhere. A "special way to be afraid", as WH Auden has it, which religion's "vast moth-eaten brocade" tried to dispel. But we will stay with the common-sense view, and use it to vanquish oblivion in a quite surprising way. Because by taking seriously and consistently the common-sense view, we will argue that there is no oblivion; that while objectively surely every body dies, subjectively we are eternal. This is subjective immortality.

Materialism and the varieties of physicalism

From the late sixth century BC, in Greece, there was a flowering of philosphy unprecedented in its breadth and duration. The lives and thoughts of Leucippus, Democritus, Zeno, Socrates, Leucippus: ~450 BC Democritus: 450-370 Epicurus: 341 - 270 one hundred years separates, in between which lived... Thales of Miletus, Parmenides and Zeno of Elea unprecedented and sustained Leucippus and his student Democritus TODO: Strong and weak emergence, downward causation (Free Will), type and token physicalism, etc.

One can close one eye and defocus the other and see the intellectual history of humanity as being the slow realisation that complex behaviour arises from simple rules. Let's picture materialism as the logical cumination of this tradition: the universe is matter and the physical laws that each particle obeys. Everything, planets and plants and mathematics and Tuesdays, emerges from that simplicity. Some features do so in a straightforward way. Atoms from elementary particles; dust from atoms; planets from dust. In Three mysteries we will argue that life is not so great a mystery. The emergence of chemistry from physics and biology from chemistry is not unimaginable. Weak emergence describes this explicable appearance. One can see planets in motes of dust, and life in a chemical reaction. We can talk about the laws of biology (Mendelian genetics, say), meaningfully, yet with the need to remember that these are weakly emergent phenomena, analogous to the way we arbitrarily divide reality into distinct objects. It is useful to speak of a table and a chair, even though were we to look at the base physical reality of bosons and fermions, any distinction between what one might sit on and what one might rest one's drink on disappears. One can always zoom down to the base reality and find elementary particles acting to precise laws, unaffected by whatever emerges from them.

This is not obviously true. Indeed most of us, most of the time, implicitly believe it to be false. Two hundred years ago Pierre-Simon Laplace described a demon who, with perfect knowledge of the current state of the universe, and of all base physical laws, could predict the future with certainty. In other words, if the base substrate of reality is composed of absolute and deterministic laws, the future is set, the entire history of the universe was decided at its inception. The clock set to midnight now will chime twelve in twelve hours time. Free will, then, is an illusion. Every decision we have ever felt we have made was already decided thirteen billion years ago.

There are a great many arguments as to why Laplace's demon cannot exist, many of them obviously flawed. To take two I ran across recently (and with apologies for the tangent). The first is from Free Will, which one might call the anti-Appointment in Sumera. It goes: imagine that the demon tells you that tomorrow you will go to the opera. On learning this, you choose to walk in the park instead. The demon is, therefore, thwarted. But while this underlines that the demon cannot be an active part of the world which it is predicting, it does not mean the demon itself could not know. The second argument is from the Halting Problem, and is simply incorrect. It is a foundational result in computer science, that owes to the young Alan Turing, that given an arbitrary program and an input to it, it is impossible to come up with a general system capable of telling whether that program will run forever or not. To give a toy example, say we have a program that purports to return the square of a number (I give it 4, it returns a nanosecond later with 16; I give it 20, it returns two nonoseconds later with 400, etc). Given some potential input to the squaring program (i.e. if we conjure up some number we think we would like squared), there is no general way to tell whether if we applied this number to the program it would ever give us a response. Programs contain bugs: perhaps this one gets trapped in a loop if asked to square a negative number, or a number greater than 1000000, and so on. The only general way to tell is run the program itself and wait until it does, in fact, halt. Which is to say, there does exist a system which can tell us whether any program which will halt will halt, and that is just to run the program. The argument against Laplace's demon is that since the demon can (assuming determinism) tell the future, it can look into the future and tell us whether a program halts or not, i.e. the demon is that general system which cannot exist. But here, how is Laplace's demon actually telling the future? It is doing the exact equivalent of running the program, where the program in this case is the entire universe! Knowing the future does not solve the halting problem. (One could add, in a finite universe the Halting Problem is always solvable, as there exists a largest number that can possibly be expressed.)

A more fruitful line of attack against the demon is indeterminism. If there are physical processes which are truly random, then it is impossible to tell the next state of a system, no matter how much you know about the current state and its physical laws. And yet, the further up the scale we go, from an electron that is a true smear of possibilities, to a human, who knows exactly where he is, the more that randomness cancels itself out. Large objects behave deterministically. We will pick up this argument later.

Even in a world with randomness at the smallest scales, Free Will looks like predestination, and causation is all one way: from the underlying fabric of the universe, up the layers of abstraction. That assumes that Free Will is a weakly emergent phenomenon. What if it were not? To say that it might be strongly emergent is to say that something truly new appears in the universe, that it might be a cause. A strongly emergent phenomenon might be able to excert downward causation, to not be the mere consequence of elementary particles dancing their dance, but change the music. One idea: while an elementary particle could equally jump in any number of directions, the mechanism of Free Will somehow chooses one possibility, biases which future is reached. I can see the attraction, and it seems to be a way to combine Free Will with materialism using an underlying randomness. Is Free Will the same as consciousness? In which case the shape of things is: there is the material universe, elementary particles operating under simple rules with an element of randomness, and as we scale up various phenonema weakly emerge (dust, rain, moons); but at a certain point, something genuinely new appears, consciousness, with the ability to effect downward change. The very fact that a particle is part of a conscious entity means it behaves differently than if it were mere matter, it swerves. It explains why our decisions have meaning, the future is predestined except when we decide.

I find the above idea neat, and difficult to believe. In attempting to save Free Will it requires a principle, downward causation, that feels impossible. How do the particles coordinate, exactly which particles are swerved? ("Swerve" is a venerable term from Epicurian physics. This is not a new idea.) We start with materialism and imagine that souls appear from nothing.

What of Free Will then, is that an illusion? The closest I can get to an answer to that is to cheat a little. There is a feeling to making a decision, of processing information, conjuring counterfactuals in one's mind, and so on. Imagine that the world is deterministic, and the future is decided, there is no Free Will in the strong sense. The feeling still exists, the decision making, the information processing, still occurs, what feels like the right decision is still made. If history were rerun you would make an identical decision, but of course you would. From high above, one can say that there can be no blame, because no one has any real option in what they choose to do, for every bad decision there was no good decision that could have been made. But we do not live on that level, and on the level at which we do live we can ask whether we are good or not -- meaning whether we exist in such a way as to make good decisions or bad. An objection: but no one chooses to be as they are. An answer: if a person does evil things, call them evil. We are what we do. When we think of a person we are not thinking of a separate entity unfortunately connected to an evil body, who could have been connected to a thoroughly admirable body were the universe to have shaken out a little differently. We are talking about that evil body, there is no other entity involved.

If Free Will could be merely the sensation of coming to a conclusion, in the same way that hunger is that of needing to be fed, then what of sensation itself? In a paper ("Emergence") David Clamers argues that consciousness is a good candidate for a strongly emergent phenomenon, perhaps the only one.

A simple argument against dualism

A diversion. After Descartes published the most famous and influential dualistic theory in his Meditations, of a material body inhabited by an immaterial soul, his correspondent Elizabeth of Bohemia asked: "Given that the soul of a human being is only a thinking substance, how can it affect the bodily spirits, in order to bring about voluntary actions?" Exactly how can the soul control the body if they are of substances which cannot interact? She argued that contact is required (in the same way Hobbes had required physical contact in his materialism), yet a spirit cannot push matter. Descartes's answer was to give an example of influence without contact: the weight of a rock moving it downwards. This was 1643; twenty two years later Newton published his theory of gravity with its spooky action at a distance firmly embedded in the material world. Fifty years after that Leibniz published his answer to the problem of how the mental can affect the physical: it doesn't. My wish to raise my arm and the physical fact that my arm rises are in a coincidental relationship, not a causal one. Simply, the universe was set up in the beginning as synchronized physical and mental realms, like two perfect clocks, set to, and started at, the same time. It is certainly a bold answer.

Descartes considered perception to be part of the mental realm, and consciousness is all perception. But we should ask: perception of what, and how? If the mental world cannot push the physical, isn't it the case that perceiving the physical involves some manner of push too? Light is photons hitting the eye, noise is vibrations within the ear. If we can wonder how the mental world affects the physical, the problem of how the physical world affects the mental is equally fraught. Either the mental can affect the physical (and be affected by it), in which case it must be like the physical world, a similar substance, not another realm. Or it cannot, in which case we can have no experience at all of the physical world. Whatever feelings we have are purely mental, wholly untethered from matter (whatever that could be). The material world disappears, and we are left with the mental one: idealism, which was Bishop Berkeley's answer to Descartes, given only a little earlier than Leibniz. In this way dualism, the idea that there are two separate yet overlapping realms, Descartes's explanation of how I think, therefore I am can be reconciled with dust, rocks, planets, and stars; in this way dualism wavers, then either collapses into materialism, or into idealism.

So how to choose between materialism and idealism? A physical realm, which is so difficult to square with the fact of consciousness, or a mental realm, in which the world disappears if we forget about it. "I refute it thus," said Dr Johnson of idealism, and kicked a rock. Idealism solves the problem of consciousness at the expense of everything else. It needs God to remember where all the pieces are -- if a tree falls in the forest and nobody is there, someone has to be there. Of idealism, no further conclusions can be drawn. Materialism gives more to play with, and we can maybe approach, if not solve, the mystery at its heart. No one needs to remember where every rock and pebble in the universe rests. They simply abide, as does everything. Falling trees may make as much noise as they like. We are starting to create a pattern here: not a conclusion so much as a choice between two extremes. The middle path is the easier to believe, and is incorrect.

Boltzmann and Nietzsche

Friedrich Nietzsche and Ludwig Boltzmann were born in the same year, 1844, and died, neither easily, six years apart. I have not managed to discover whether the German philospher and the Austrian mathematician and physicist ever met or read one another. Boltzmann was the more influential: he created modern thermodynamics. Nietzsche ultimately the more famous: he said that God is Dead. And he struggled with the Eternal Return, an old idea, Heraclitus' Great Year, that the universe has existed forever in an endless cycle, every moment repeated to infinity. (A sketch of a sophistic proof: you exist in this moment. If the universe has always existed, if there were no moment of creation, then the beginning of the universe is literally infinitely distant. In order to reach the present moment an infinite amount of time must have passed, yet how can that be? By definition that never happens, the present must always be in the future. Now imagine that the universe repeats itself, and does so exactly. There is no longer any need to wait an infinite amount of time to reach now, as this moment occurs and recurs. An Eternal Return solves the problem of a universe with no beginning.) His struggle was how to believe that each life is repeated endlessly, and not despair. It is a sort of subjective immortality, one that feels like mortal life. How to "long for nothing more ardently" than this "eternal sanctioning and sealing"? The world is "a musical mechanism [that] repeats eternally its tune, which must never be called a melody."

One could imagine a dualistic version of Eternal Return in which the same events are endlessly replayed yet the souls are different each time. A person just like me has already lived a life just like mine, like actors taking the same role. Subjectively then, each person would live only one life, and we return to the paradox of how long to wait until it is you inhabiting this body, if the universe had no beginning. But this is not Nietzsche's idea. One could say too that it assumes a deterministic universe. Though even were physics indeterministic, eventually every possibility would be exhausted, and repeated, too.

Some words of Nietzsche's could fit Boltzmann: "The general character of the world [...] is to all eternity chaos". Boltzmann did not discover the Second Law of Thermodynamics, which can be stated many ways, one of them being: heat flows from hot to cold, and requires work to flow in the opposite direction. Your teacup warms the room and cools. The fact of it was discovered by the first living things, the law by Carnot, Clausius, and Kelvin. Ludwig Boltzmann explained why, in statistical terms, it must be true. His is the modern definition of entropy, the modern understanding of why, for example, it is perfectly possible for heat to flow backwards, from cold to hot, just very, very unlikely. This melancholy man offered two conceptions of the universe, one in which the universe began in a low-entropy state (a version of which would later be called the Big Bang), and another that was more fiery even than Nietzsche's: total chaos. A universe that has existed forever without sense or plan, a universe of maximal entropy. On its face that is absurd because here we are, calmly circling a yellow star, with order wherever we look. But that gets to the fact that heat will flow backwards if you wait long enough, that if you throw enough dice you will find any number of 6s you choose. The very fact that it is random means that structures will spontaneously appear, then disspate. Our universe as a statistical quirk.

This idea was later lampooned as the "Boltzmann Brain": that, far more likely than the whole universe appearing on the flip of a coin, is that a single brain appears, with enough time to have one thought (the thought you are thinking right now), before it falls apart, utterly untenable that its existence is. Current physicists generally want to avoid Boltzmann Brains, it is too easy to argue that given the infinite future of the universe, we are each far more likely to be momentary Boltzmann Brains at the eternal end of the universe than real people towards its beginning. The Boltzmann universe is like a crazed, random version of Nietzsche's Eternal Return. Everything will happen and none of it has an obligation to make sense. Boltzmann Brains haunt theories of the distant future like ghosts in a country house.

Functionalism (are we nothing but the pattern?)

"Why may we not say, that all Automata ... have an artificiall life? ... What is the Heart but a Spring; and the Nerves, but so many Strings?" asks Hobbes in the introduction to his Leviathan. He uses this idea as a metaphor and more than a metaphor to describe the State, to say that there is something not merely analogously but genuinely alive about a well-ordered Commonwealth. It is a glimmer of a computational theory of the mind, made bright as an arc-light by Turing three hundred years later. The interesting thing about computation is it is the symbols and the rules through which they are processed that count, not whatever physical machine actually carries out the processing; and the other interesting thing is that "computable" is a curiously stable and fixed concept. If a problem is, in principle, possible to compute using one simple machine, it turns out to be computable by a very different one (albeit faster or slower). Computers can use binary or ternary or quantum logic, can offer a thousand baroque instructions in which to compose programs or just one, can be mechanical or biological, can even be composed of pebbles on an infinite beach with rules for how to move them, and be able to solve the exact same set of problems. "Computable" is a thing apart from the computer. Functionalism can be viewed in this light: a game of chess played with pieces made from glass is the same game as one played with pieces made of wood. What matters is the actions of the pieces (that the bishop is moved this way, the king that), and the relations between them, not whatever physical substrate the game runs on. The computations, not the computer. An attempt to separate what is real from what is contingent.

And if our brains are something like computers, and consciousness is like a computation, then the fact that this computation is carried out by a web of neurons is not the important fact. A lot of people, especially those over the bay from my present Berkeley home, in San Francisco, have got very excited by this idea. Flesh is temporary, and though against the evidence of actual machines, silicon could be forever. If functionalism were true, and a sufficiently faithful copy of a person could be uploaded to a machine, then that copy would be that person, in all ways that matter. What we mean by functionalism being true is that were we to do this, the consciousness of the person being uploaded would continue. Their genuine experience would be life, then a continued life, forever perhaps, and maybe even good (though good and bad would be at the whim of whoever had control over the new substrate on which this person runs. If I believed it were possible, I have enough imagination to know never to consent to it). Of course this is unfalsifiable. Not even the person being uploaded could ever truly know. Either the person no longer exists, and the simulated copy is no more conscious than a character in a computer game, in which case oblivion is all and there is no one to know; or the simulated copy has a genuine consciousness too, and has the memories of that copied person and belief that they might be them. But they could not really know, their memories are a construct. If their testimony could not solve it (believing that testimony requires assuming it to be true, it is circular reasoning), what objective test could possibly be done?

It is worth returning to the assertion that even if true, the person could not know, because it will give a hint at our direction of travel. Is there really not a moment in which they would know? That bridge from a human about to undergo whatever horribly destructive slicing scanning procedure is required, and a machine intelligence in its new nirvana. (Picture a Bay Area computer scientist's idea of heaven. Forever.) Either there is something, some next moment for that person, or nothing. But I do not think so. Think of this moment right now; for you, what was the bridge from the previous moment? Only your short-term memory. If you and the universe came into being exactly now, you could not possibly know it. Every moment of consciousness is new, a start, a link in a chain whose past existence we have to assume.

Functionalism can look like wishful thinking amongst a class of people trained in abstractions. "Surely not everything has to matter," it says, "there has to be some simpler, cleaner form, divorced from the contingencies of history. Mathematics does not care if you add oranges or apples, why can't everything be like that?" To which one reasonable answer might be: mathematics is exactly that part of the universe for which this can be done, and equations are not conscious. Whenever a computer scientist invokes "Turing Machines" in the same sentence as the word "consciousness", which is frequently, look askance. We could defend the position by observing a whirlpool in our bath and a whirlpool galaxy; that a computer simulation of birds flocking, embodying a few simple rules, looks very like the real thing. More than thirty years ago, the computer program Tierra demonstrated evolution in a box. Is consciousness like flocking, a general thing that can equally be embodied by birds or fish or as electrons flowing through logic gates in silicon; or is it like identity? I am only truly equal to myself.

If consciousness is a sort of pattern, a relationship between things for which the things themselves are purely contingent, then a proof of subjective immortality is straightforward. Your consciousness, your subjective experience, right now, is a pattern made of the matter of your body. The pattern is a moving thing, it evolves through time; an experience is a certain sort of flux. Think, now, of the far future. Whenever another group of things exhibits that same pattern, there you will be again. Perhaps as a creature who looks much like you; perhaps as complex swirls and counter-swirls of dust on a distant planet; perhaps as a computer program running an endless game world; or even, immensely slowly, as the stars themselves, spelling out the steps of your subjective experience from the perspective of one sullen moon. Who can say? In an infinite universe, every pattern will be repeated, and every person is a pattern. There is no escape, and yet is there need for one?

Functionalism does provide a relatively straightforward proof of subjective immortality. It is an unsatisfying one though. Intellectually it misses a key point of abstractions: that they leave important things out. Technologists who abstract away details of the machines on which their frequently meretricious software platforms run have to unabstract them in a hurry whenever they hit performance bottlenecks or RAM gets expensive again. And it mistakes analogy for fact. A human brain is not a computer; neural nets were inspired by our brains (and predate much of computer science), but function absolutely nothing like them. The simulation of a flock of birds is not a flock of birds. There is no there there for a chatbot, for all that it insists that there is. It is not even lying.

Quantum suicide

There is a cute thought experiment, originally published in the 1980s by Hans Moravec, and given here in a form that owes to Max Tegmark ten years later. It begins with Schrödinger's famous cat in a box and then asks, what if we are the cat? Outside is a button, which pressed may kill you instantly with a perfectly random 50/50 chance. It is pressed many times, what do you experience?

The experiment serves a double purpose. Its first is to furnish a proof of the Many Worlds interpretation of quantum physics, as opposed to the Copenhagen interpretation. At each measurement, does the universe split in two? Do all possible futures in a real sense exist, and do so simultaneously? While in terms of the sheer volume of what it implies, it is the least parsimonious theory of reality imaginable, it does make some things simpler. So what happens if the button is pressed multiple times and the Many Worlds hypothesis is true? You are still alive. With each press the likelihood of your continued existence halves, and yet, no matter how many times your presumed enemy stabs the death button, you abide, unpoisoned, safe in your unsafe box. Why? Because there is always a future in which you are alive, and it is the future in which you are alive. So of course, the you who can experience anything is the you who experiences the repeated failure to kill you. Any objective observer will be overwhelmingly likely to see you die (apart from that one observer in the narrowing reality in which you survive every attempt on your life). This is why as a proof of Many Worlds it rather falls apart; even in the universe in which you are a witness to your own amazing survival, it is far more likely the aparatus had malfunctioned, or your enemy had a soft spot for you after all. Objectively you are as mortal as ever, even as you subjectively continue -- or one of you does.

The second purpose of the experiment is to ask: what if you are not in a box, does that alter the experiment? The cat in the box is in a quantum superposition; you are merely sitting in a dangerous box. Rather than a controlled environment with a 50/50 chance of death, think of normal life. When you are young, your chances of dying on any given day are small; when old, larger; when very old, overwhelmingly so. And yet there is always a chance you will survive, that a blood vessel would hold, that a host of bacteria might spontaneously die. Every conscious moment contains the chance for another conscious moment, and if all possibilities are explored, then the ones you will experience are exactly those in which your survival, however bizarre and unlikely as the years, decades, centuries progress, extends.

A first thought should be: what an awful fate we all have. If true, we are each Tithonus, with infinite life yet without infinite youth. Quantum suicide is a horrifying idea. A second thought is: if truly all possible next states are explored, then what about the one in which you are the same age or slightly younger than you are now? There is a sequence of moments, physically possible and fantastically unlikely, in which you remain yourself, unaging and undying. Consider a brain cell. It will lose action, weaken, then die, over the course of your life. But in any given moment, it might remain perfectly healthy. As the future of the Multiverse branches crazily out, there are always branches in which you are safe, whole, and well. Are we saved from Tithonus' fate, then?

I am 47, neither as fit nor healthy as I was 20 years ago. Maybe a little wiser. It does not appear that I have been picking the non-aging option offered by the Multiverse. Or rather, I am not that one of me. Because the logic of Quantum suicide is that in every moment we are copied an unimaginable number of times, and each of these copies continues their own lives, each with the same history, each with their own future. The me who lives forever young is one of them, as are countless versions of Tithonus. Most of these lines end though, it is not a proof against oblivion. To imagine otherwise is to conjure a self able to skip between universes.

Take the idea at face value, the Many Worlds hypothesis is real, from this moment all possible next moments do exist, each their own new universe. In the present moment you are alive. In the next moment, there are countless universes in which you are dead, yet always others in which you are alive, even for just one moment which becomes just another moment and so on. There is a chance of immortality. Also a chance of oblivion, and the universes in which you do not exist will come to vastly outnumber those in which you do. Why should you happen to continue in one of those universes, what is the mechanism? We have already decided that there is no extra-physical property, no soul that could flit from one reality to another. In fact, the chances of you being immortal if Many Worlds is true is exactly the same as the chances of you being immortal if it is false, and there is only one reality, one next moment from this one. From every moment there is always a chance of survival, after all.

We can simplify the situation to make this clearer. Think of the original formulation again, of a box, with a button outside, and a 50/50 chance of death each time it is pressed. Say it is pressed 4 times. In the Many Worlds situation, we begin with one world, then two with the first button press, then four with the second, eight with the third, and sixteen by the fourth. In fifteen of the sixteen worlds you are dead. Quantum suicide wants to say that the chances of you being alive and conscious at the end of this process is a certainty: since at each step there is only one option in which your consciousness can continue, that is the option in which it does. But why is the likelihood not 1/16? Why is it not exactly the same, indeed, as if Many Worlds were false?

We can try to sharpen this question, as we will return to the intuitions on "subjective lines" later, by offering a less morbid variation of the problem. It is February 28th as I type this, and you are somewhere, you exist. Since then, possibilities have exploded outwards, there is a world in which, by now, you are in France, and another in which you are in Spain, and another in which, by some fantastical sequence of events you are currently orbiting the Earth at an altitude of 400 kilometres. There are countless options of what you could, by now, be experiencing. You are only conscious of one of these options though, the universe in which we both live. The other leg of the trousers of time (in Pratchett's coinage) is a mystery to you, you are the one who went in this direction. The rhetorical trick Quantum Suicide tries to pull is making it appear that there is only one option for one's consciousness to "go". Death cuts off one leg of the Trousers of Time. Why should it though? And what, really, has moved?

Note that it still works as an empirical proof of Many Worlds! One of the possible yous really will always be alive, no matter how many times the button is pressed (that is, no matter how many other possible yous have been killed). But it does not work as an argument for personal immortality.

Consider another variation of the experiment. You are still in a box, though this time the button controls a more benign mechanism. It merely sends you immediately into a dreamless sleep. The button is pressed: in one reality, you are conscious, in the other reality, you are unconscious. In one reality, that is, your subjective experience exists, while in the other it does not. The choice, again, is between existence or non-existence (albeit of a temporary kind). Your conscious self can only be, by definition, in the reality in which you are conscious. So there it goes. The button is pressed again, and again there is a choice, where should your conscious self go? The same logic pertains, and we might conclude that the subjective experience of being inside this sleeping experiment is to always remain awake (even as any experimenter will genuinely see you fall asleep for 50% of button presses). Does this idea also transfer to the world outside of the box? At any moment that you become conscious, there is a chance you will remain awake, meaning a universe in which you remain awake. And, by what is, recall, similar logic to the case in which you both die and remain alive, surely your consciousness should always follow into the universe in which you remain conscious?

We could ask: does this imply that none of us should ever become unconscious, in the same way that Quantum suicide might imply that none of us should ever subjectively die? Almost, in fact yes. If there is some unit of consciousness flowing from one moment to the next, from one possibility to the next, then whenever we emerge from unconsciousness a new unit must be created, as the old one already exists, and is in the universe in which it is already conscious. If so, this is not much less to be feared than our comparison to Tithonus above. It implies that while you might remember falling unconscious in the past, that was not you, it was a previous conscious entity, you are a new one, and you will never in the future fall unconscious, you will always be in that reality in which you remain conscious, forever.

Where is the error? That there is some conscious unit or entity "going" from one place to the next. The error is nothing to do with this or that interpretation of quantum physics. The error is a sneaking dualism.

An inkling of the problem of personal immortality

We make a mistake if we think of personal immortality, of an infinity of separate chains of consciousness, wending in parellel through the universe, meeting perhaps more than once across awful spans of time. It is a mistake because implicit is the stubborn idea that body and consciousness are separate, and the body changes while our conscious self, against all objective evidence, does not. We are haunted by the soul.

A certain sort of philosophy is built from merely slightly tricky ideas, which compounded by very tricky arguments lead to wholly tricky conclusions. Gottfried Leibniz, who answered Elizabeth of Bohemia's problem so audaciously in an earlier chapter, was a wonder for such ideas: the principle of sufficient reason, the law of continuity, the identity of indiscernibles... The indiscernibility of identicals (called Leibniz's Law) is about as uncontroversial an assertion as one can find: if two things are the same, then everything that is true about one of them is true for the other (if A is a red ball, and B = A, then B must be red, and a ball). The identity of indiscernibles asserts that the converse holds too: that if everything true of one thing is true of another thing, they must in fact be the same thing. This underlines another feature of this sort of philosphy: because on its face the identity of indiscernibles is obviously, trivially true (if A is a red ball, and B is a red ball, and A's position in space and time is exactly the same as B's position in space and time, then of course A = B), then the identity of indiscernibles is usually phrased in a way to give it a chance of being false (A and B are both red balls, do not ask where each is though), without also being clearly so. It is relevant here because we will present various arguments which rely on it being false, of us constructing two individuals identical in every way that are none the less distinct. It is a caution of the type of philosophizing I wish to avoid though, a principle grounded in logic (and, more than anything, in language) which tries to say something about the physical universe, that is under no obligation to listen. Are any two electrons identical? So far, all experimental evidence appears to suggest that they are -- electrons are identical in the sense of being interchangable, swapping electrons between atoms would result in no observable change to the atoms. That would be enough to falsify the identity of indiscernibles, and is a result that makes my arguments easier. In this book I will try to keep my arguments clear and straightforward, and when I make an assumption about the world (that, in this example, fundamental particles are identical), which is generally or only generally believed to be true, I will call it out.

Hobbes's Ship of Theseus

The story is about a ship. But I want to remember a tree, a maple in my back garden. In the early morning of Christmas a storm brought down a branch from a nearby redwood which, as large as a narrowboat, destroyed a fence and also my maple. Before the remains of the dead tree were chainsawed then chipped, I cut a tiny branch the length of a hand and tinner than a finger, dipped it in rooting powder, then planted it in a small pot of watered soil. Remarkably, the branch sprouted and grew. It is still small as I write; in twenty years it will be as big as the tree it came from. To what extent is it that original tree, or a clone, or a new tree? Would the question have a different answer were it grown from a larger branch? Plants show how fuzzy the bounds of personal identity can be.

How old is a bacterium? Say it divides every 20 minutes, does that mean it is at most 20 minutes old? Or, if we follow one bacterium, and then one of the two bacteria it becomes, and one that that becomes, and so on, could the same bacterium be said to reproduce itself, then reproduce itself again? In splitting preserving some fundamental part of itself, beyond its rings of DNA. In which case, even if it is unlikely to live much longer (and most bacteria die quickly or the world would be full), it can be said to have lived for three and a half billion years, all the way back to the first life on Earth. We animals separate our potentially immortal part, the germline, from our bodies; or rather, we add a disposable body to what the bacteria and archaea are in their entirety. Plants and fungi are similar to animals (everything we can see with the naked eye is like us), though as with my maple, plants, lacking organs as such, and with a more generous attitude to stem cells, have lives more salvagable then ours. That obvious thought, that personal continuity is a fact of nature, has frayed edges. To try to draw a sharp line around anyone is to do exactly that: make a choice on where the line begins and ends, what it encompasses, what is in and what is out, what is you and what is the world.

If there were something like being a bacterium, not sight or sound as it has neither sense, although there are light-sensitive bacteria; an electric potential, a salt concentration, a measurable property that must be maintained; if, however slight, a bacterium could be a centre of subjective experience (and surely the experience itself would feel like the whole world), then that could represent billions of years of subjective continuity, if subjective continuity exists. Immortal, until the present moment (aren't we all?). And a subjective continuity that multiplies itself. A subjective continuity that looks a oddly like the branching we discussed in reference to Quantum Suicide. Is there, though? Does subjective experience appear once a nervous system gets complex enough, like the ability to run away or learn Latin? And appear from what?

Time to talk about the Ship of Theseus. When he returned from Minos having killed the Minotaur and rescued the brightest young of Athens, Theseus was meant to fly white sails, not the usual black, to signal to his father the king that he had survived. His father, impatiently one might say, threw himself into the sea in despair as soon as he saw black sails, and Theseus became king. His ship was kept seaworthy so each year it could make a trip to Delos in honor of Apollo, patron of Theseus' success. It is not recorded what colour sails the ship flew, but it is known that over the decades as one plank rotted it was replaced with another, so that the ship itself survived for centuries. Wood rotted fast with bronze-age technology, so after a few decades every single plank had been replaced more than once, not a mote of the original remained. Yet there was no point at which an observer would have said: at this repair the old ship has gone, and what we have is a mere copy.

A common thought experiment: imagine your neurons are replaced one-by-one with exact copies in silicon. These chips function in just the same way, and only differ in their durability. After a few dozen neurons are replaced, you would presumably still feel like you. As more and more of your brain is "upgraded", could there come a tipping point at which you would feel less like yourself; or would there be a creeping lack of feeling? Would there be any change at all? By definition (almost), you would continue to behave the same, since the silicon neurons continue to function as your feeble carbon ones did. After 51% or 99% of your brain is replaced, would your consciousness wink out of existence, even as you continue to say that all is well? A philosphical zombie (or almost one; and in the chapter on Continuities, we will argue is impossible). What the Ship of Theseus encourages us to believe is that it matters that the change is done gradually. A ship replaced plank-by-plank can be the same ship in a way that if we were to copy Theseus' ship outright it would not be: to replace over time is not to copy. If we apply the same intuition to our silicon brain, then it is almost easy to believe that were your neurons replaced slowly enough whatever it is that is you can be preserved; and also that if a copy is made in silicon of your brain, it would be a mere copy, and not you.

A pure functionalist would say that of course the copy in silicon is you -- if its functioning is identical, your consciousness is realized in another form. It should not count one way or the other whether the replacement is undertaken slowly or quickly or all at once. The body is not a cage for the soul that, if broken, lets it fly free, never to return. A materialist would not be so emphatic: the material is, after all, very different, and who is to say what function, exactly, is important. Your human brain weighs one and a half kilos, its silicon equivalent weighs five kilos (say). Is weight important to the appearance or otherwise of consciousness, of a subjective experience and of your subjective experience in particular? It would be surprising, though what isn't? Perhaps only carbon atoms exhibit the glimmers of proto-feeling of which, scaled past some threshold, constitute the thereness of a human being. Perhaps it is our gut bacteria and no eucaryotic cell is conscious in the slightest, what a twist.

A version of the argument for materialists is one in which the matter within your skull is replaced by equivalent matter, atom by atom. There is no abstraction, no need to define the necessary and sufficient features which, if present, create feeling in the world. The you at the start of the process and the you at the end, once every one of your atoms has been swapped out, oxygen for oxygen, carbon for carbon, and so on, you appear to be exactly the same. To reiterate the thought experiment for materialists: imagine a person A, who is conscious, and whose consciousness we denote by a. This person changes through time, and we use a simple subscript to denote this, so A_0 is the person at the start time 0, while a_1 represents their consciousness at time 1. Now, imagine that we could start with A_0 and very slowly replace every atom with equivalent atoms, but crucially in such a way that does not interfer with A's normal life (so, whatever A chooses to do during their day is unaffected by this stealthy process of atom replacement. Imagine some fantastical and discreet transporter technology). Denote this updated entity by B. At time 0, we have not replaced any atoms, so clearly A_0 = B_0. By time 4 (say), half the atoms have been swapped out, so we cannot say that A_4 = B_4, as they do differ. By time 10, all of the atoms have been replaced. However, if we were to try and tell the difference between A_10 and B_10, how would we do it? The two entities do not differ in any physically testable way (we can make this argument stronger by considering not atoms but fundemantal particles of which our atoms are formed; we have already mentioned that all electrons are identical). Considering the subjective experiences of each entity, a_10 and b_10, if they differ (if they are the subjective experiences of different people), where is that difference expressed? If physically indistinguishable, a theory like materialism or physicalism that claims that physical reality is the only reality must have any distinction be a physical distinction. If we cannot find a physical difference between two things, those two things are the same thing: a_10 = b_10. Again, it is neither here nor there how quick or slow the transition is. And we have the equivalent argument if a person A is copied exactly to produce a person B. Think, now, if you were copied right now. Which of the copies would be you? A materialist should say: both, because any real difference must be told in a physical way.

If we take physicalism seriously, we have to assume that both gradual replacement and an immediate copy result in the same situation. Either both are still "you", or neither are. To underline the reasoning: if there is a difference between them now, in the present (that difference being: one creates your consciousness, and the other does not), then since the physical world is all that there is, that difference must be physical. Where is the difference though, between a copy made gradually, and the same copy made all at once? Their histories are different, yet we can assume that their present physical forms are identical, and we are looking for a difference in the present moment. We will return to this idea.

There is an escape if otherwise identical atoms exhibit some inner difference. If, for example, each atom really does have its own unique form of subjective experience, so even if outwardly identical, atoms are not interchangeable. This subjective experience would need to have no causal effects though (or it would be an outward difference) -- and this is a brazen dualistic coup. We start by saying we need to do away with the soul/body distinction, there is no spiritual world, only the physical; and we end up suggesting that every atom has a soul. We might return to this idea if forced into it. For now, we should try not to give up materialism to panpsychism.

The Ship of Theseus has proved useful mainly as an example of a trap of which we should be aware, an intuition about the importance of the history of an object that physicalism dissolves. Continuity is overrated. Why Hobbes's Ship of Theseus though? Because Thomas Hobbes was the one who thought about collecting the old planks of the ship as they are being repaired and creating a new ship out of them. So there are two ships, one composed entirely of the original ship's matter, but with no proper continuity with the original ship, and the other continuous with the original ship, yet not containing any of its original matter. Which is the real ship?

The moral consequences of materialism

At a lecture recently I heard the following argument against the type of strict, reductive materialism that forms the basis of the arguments in this book. The assumption it makes is that we lose the idea of a person, travelling through time. You are conscious in this moment, here you are; yet you are produced by this particular arrangement of matter, and in the next moment that arrangement will have changed. If there is no soul or anything analogous to a soul, nothing tying the moments of your life together other than this Ship-of-Theseus of a body, then it is difficult to find that person moving through time. You are many people. (Put you as a child of 8 and you as an old person of 80 together in a room. Would the two recognise each other as the same person?) Why this has a bearing on morality is, perhaps, best explained by the Dentist Problem. Which is, simply, that sending a person to the dentist is unpleasant for them at the time, yet saves them from greater unpleasantness in the future. If the same person receives the future benefit of the present unpleasantness then there is no moral problem. But what if the person to benefit were not the person who suffers? What if you could send someone else to the dentist on your behalf and still end up with plaque-free teeth? You would profit from the suffering of someone else, someone you have decided to make suffer, and what better definition of immorality is there? If, the argument goes, a person is not some persistent thing through time; if a person is instead a flickering sequence of people, then it becomes impossible to decide to do anything without being immoral. To make an appointment to see the dentist tomorrow is to condemn one person (whatever you have become by tomorrow) to suffer, for the benefit of the people you become afterwards.

To reiterate the meat of the argument: it is immoral to harm one person to the benefit of another. The only way it can be moral to inflict harm is if, in some sense, the person would be better off for it (to push someone into the road is immoral; to push someone into the road to save them from being crushed by a falling piano several seconds later, is heroic). To be cruel to be kind. Given the logic of materialism, we lose the ability to balance a present harm with a future benefit, as the future benefit would apply to someone else, a new person (as in every moment we are in a true sense, new).

We can quibble about this argument. When I decide to go to the dentist tomorrow, I am not benefiting from it. In fact I am harmed in anticipation of that unpleasant half hour. Nevertheless, one version of me pays, and a different version profits. Could we say that this is a category error? Morality is not a feature of the universe, it is an evolved aspect of complex, social animals. A wasp is neither moral nor immoral, it merely is. Human morality assumes personal continuity, and continues to assume it whatever personal intellectual feelings we might have on the matter. I both claim to be a moral person, it is my nature; and also think that on balance reductive materialism is probably true, which does not and cannot change my nature, any more than I could decide to grow wings. The contradiction would only destroy morality if morality were like mathematics, and vulnerable to contradiction. But morality is like hunger, or love: real feelings that evolved bodies give rise to, and which have no independent existence.

Parfit's Non-Identity problem, his Repugnant Conclusion, and the Lonely Conclusion

Are future generations harmed when present generations pollute the world? Consider two versions of the future, one in which we in the present know that we are degrading our environment, and we do something about it; and another version, in which we know that we are degrading our environment, and we choose to degrade it further. (Which future we are currently creating is left as an exercise to the reader.) Consider a young person born in each future. They might live in a polluted world, or a healthy one. They are certainly better off in the healthy world. And yet, can we say that we, in the present, have harmed them, in the future? That young person in a polluted world is someone different to the young person in a healthy one. We are each a random permutation of our parents' genes, contingent; if things were even slightly different at our conception someone else would have been born, a sibling instead of not us. So the person living in a healthy world is undoubtedly better off than the person living in a polluted one, but were the world not polluted, that person would never have been born. The choice for them is not: pollution or health, it is to exist or not exist. So long as the world is not a complete hell, it is better to exist than not; so it is difficult to say that in allowing this person to exist, we have disadvantaged them. This is Parfit's Non-Identity Problem. Our moral obligation to those who do not yet exist is confused.

A solution is to fall back to the Utilitarianism of Jeremy Bentham and John Stuart Mill: it is the greatest happiness for the greatest number that matters. Happiness and number now cause us a problem. Because a trillion people whose lives are barely worth living is preferable to a million with wonderful lives: the total amount of happiness is greater, even as each person contributes only a little. Picture an Eden for a few; or a world turned into one endless housing block, with choked skies and dead ground. That is Parfit's Repugnant Conclusion. Somehow one can increase misery and make the world better.

A solution to that second problem is to consider not the total amount of happiness but the average amount. It is easy to find a counter example here though: a world in which there is one stupendously happy king with a hundred unhappy slaves could be better than a world of 101 merely happy people, were the king to enjoy himself a sufficient amount. We can be a little more sophisticated and consider the median amount of happiness: a world is good if the wellbeing of its typical citizen is good. (Think of John Rawls's Veil of Ignorance. A good world is one in which you would be happy to be born as a random person.) Here, too, it is easy to find what looks like a paradox: imagine we have a good world with 10 people in it. All the people are happy. They are not identically happy though, one is the least happy. The world of nine people, with that least happy person removed, has higher median happiness and is therefore better. The same logic pertains, and we have to conclude that in any world, with any number of people, it is better to have fewer people, all the way until there is just a single person left. Call this the Lonely Conclusion.

One objection to the Lonely Conclusion is that a person on their own is not going to be happy; another is that happiness is not a static thing, the unhappiest person today might be the happiest tomorrow; another that a single person on their own will eventually die, and not be replaced, and the median of no one is zero. I do not think that these work to explode the Conclusion though. I suspect that median happiness is the best we can do when we want to judge whether a world is good or not. But surely a world with more people is better than one with fewer? (Better for whom? Better for them!) I will argue that this line of thinking is a mistake, that the Non-Identity Problem disappears if we take physicalism seriously enough. The best world really is one in which there is a single maximally happy entity. A million people living in Arcadia is better than a trillion people existing in a towerblock 60 kilometres wide and ten kilometres high. No one is lacking, no one waits to be born.

It is not what you think

What is normal is a poor guide to the underlying nature of consciousness. That thereness must involve a) a sense of self, b) of self-protection, c) of language, d) of rationality, e) of personal continuity, are all contradicted by one mental aberration or another. Those suffering from Cotard's Syndrome believe that they are dead; those with Anton syndrome are blind, yet convinced in a way that is immune to all evidence that they can see. When the corpus callosum, the bundle of nerves that connects the hemispheres of the brain, is severed, a single consciousness collapses into two independent selves, sharing a body, and yet each having no inner knowledge of the other. There once was a king who believed he was made of glass, and another who thought he was a farmer. It is difficult to conjure a condition bizarre enough to have not been suffered by someone. Strange examples of subjective experience abound, and indeed might be the typical subjective experience of another animal. To be an octopus with its brain distributed through eight limbs and a shape-shifting skin is to be something more than a single self. A loss of language brings us closer to the other great apes in that one respect. Thomas Nagel famously asked "What is it like to be a bat?" Perfectly normal, is what it is like -- for a bat. A commonplace thought is whether the blue I see is the blue you see. How fast does the world move, do you sit in the same place behind the eyes as I do? Even around the same dinner table are different worlds.

It is unsurprising why a sense of self should be common: it fosters bodily integrity, which in turn makes it more likely that the genes for a sense of self will be replicated into the next generation. The sense of self is, then, an aspect of a sense of self-preservation. Whenever asserting these evolutionary just-so stories we ought pause and ask, are we begging the question? As evidence we could point to those experiencing drug-induced ego death as being, at the time, indisposed to making sensible long-term decisions. There are, to put it mildly, confounding factors to that example. The fact that the self can be dissolved does suggest that it is not fundamental, that it might be an invention atop the underlying machinery of experience. Vision is a common subjective experience but not a necessary one. One can exist without sight, and have a subjective experiences that are impossible for a sighted person to imagine, who can only picture black when asked to picture nothing at all. The sense of a single self, moving through time, responsible to that self in the present and in the future, is perhaps then a marvellous invention, sustained moment-by-moment in a process that only becomes apparent when it malfunctions, and then, is often only apparent to someone else.

One could draw an analogy with morality, taking up the argument from our discussion of morality and reductive materialism. It exists, and is explicable evolutionarily. Arguing that morality evolved is not the same as arguing it away. Hunger and thirst are certainly evolved sensations, hunger one of the first to appear (thirst presumably waiting until animals reached dry land), and what is more real than hunger? The gods do not need to tell us what is right and wrong for right and wrong to be, in a meaningful sense, real. (As a wild tangent, on Euthyphro’s dilemma, whether a thing is just because the gods will it, or the gods only will what is just, has an obvious answer. If the gods exist, they get to decide.) It is here, within human society, where we exist. I am not being entirely flippant when I say that to be a moral person is rather like having a liking for coffee: an underlying nature together with an upbringing in society creates a way of being. Morality exists nowhere else in the universe: rocks and suns and clouds of gas are not moral beings. There are glimmers in simpler animals, and it is from those glimmers that our morality was formed. And so with consciousness and a sense of self: it might be made from something with no sense of self. By consciousness then I mean the fact of feeling, or thereness -- qualia is the technical term. The experience before the experiencer. Consciousness is the first problem and perhaps it will be the last.

Three mysteries

Consider three mysteries: life, intelligence, and consciousness. The mystery of life is how chemistry could become biology. How did it begin, in some deep sea vent or warm sheltered pond? What were the precursors of DNA, what was the first replicator, when did evolution begin? The mystery of intelligence is finding renewed interest today: are generative artificial intelligence models exhibiting true intelligence, or merely copying from training data vast enough to encompass all every-day tasks? Is that what intelligence in fact is: finding structure in data, then applying it? Are we mere prediction machines too, or is there some qualitative difference between our intelligence and what is possible of a machine? And finally consciousness, whose mystery needs no examples.

The mysteries of life and of intelligence are readily solveable. Not easily: it has taken human civilization almost until the present day to even pose the questions in ways that admit solutions. For life, there are plausible models of how, for example, submarine alkaline vents of the early Earth could have contained cell-like structures and harboured life-like chemistry that birthed the ancestors of bacteria and archaea. Experiments can be run, mathematical models constructed. The emergence of life may be so unlikely that we will have to imagine a trillion Earth-like planets before it becomes a possibility. An explanation that makes life almost inevitable would be easier to believe (and have more worrying implications). Perhaps we will go to the moons of Jupiter or Saturn to look for evidence of a parallel creation. We will never know the precise moment or location of life's appearance on Earth, nor exactly what that first cell looked like, how long it lasted, whether there was a moment in which a single living thing existed in all the universe. (The tiny cell is forlorn / void of the little living will / that made it stir on the shore. Tennyson.) But that is not necessary to feel that we have solved the mystery of life. We need a narrative of how, step by step, chemistry could become biology, one that makes testable hypothoses, and allows for its steps to be repeated, either in simulation or directly in a laboratory. And if the universe really is predisposed to life, we will, eventually, find it out there too; and if the path is so narrow that in most galaxies not a single planet will give rise to life, then as the centuries and millennia pass, the further we travel the more confident we will be in saying: we are alone with ourselves. The mystery is in finding the right arrangement of pieces that we already have. It requires many small new ideas, yet no revolution in understanding. Those big ideas have already been had.

And frequently, in the solution to the mystery of life, we will assert things like: carbon chemistry is such that it allows for vastly more complex molecules than can be constructed with other atoms as their base. Or, a proton gradient is built as a store of energy which is then used to power this or that reaction. One can go a step deeper, look at the electron structure of carbon atoms, say; study how semipermeable lipid membranes interact with free hydrogen atoms. It is not necessary though to feel satisfied. The properties of the physical universe are facts. Their own mysteries perhaps -- yet you do not have to solve everything in order to solve one thing. That the physical world has certain predictable physical properties is easily given. We already live in a world of physical laws.

Intelligence has similar properties as a problem, though is if anything more empirical. Is intelligence being able to win a game of chess? Yes -- until we managed to write computer programs which will reliably beat any human chess player who ever lived, and then no. As isolated human skills fall to software, we do get closer to an idea of what that general intelligence might be. The ability to learn from just a few examples (not the entire Internet); to hold ideas, and combine them in novel ways; to have a memory that is, in a sense, alive. None of this necessarily requires consciousness, and it is plausible (and literally trillions of dollars are, as I write, riding on this proposition) that this decade we will have software systems which exceed any type of human intelligence we can, with our human intelligence, find. A neural net of sufficient scale, of the right sort of complexity? There need be nothing like to be that machine, no further mystery. Intelligence seems to be a problem of data manipulation, and ninety years into the Comupter Age there are few mysteries left on that score.

The mystery of consciousness is different. An active area of study is to find the neural correlates of consciousness: exactly those structures and signals that mean a brain is conscious. If one can see this synchronized wave across the brain at this frequency, the person is conscious; otherwise not. Create that situation, and you have created a conscious experience. The short term promise is to be able to tell whether, say, a person in a persistent vegetative state can in fact feel, locked inside their head; the long term promise is to be able to create new consciousness itself. It is Promethean, Frankensteinian even, the ability to create life, intelligent life, conscious life. Yet even then, a mystery will remain, an itch of a why. We could have complete operational control over the nature of consciousness, and still not know how it can be that an experience can exist, more than matter and more than data. We can solve life and intelligence from the outside, because we experience them from the outside. We are conscious from the inside.

How to explain subjective experience? That for a lump of matter there is a there there, a window on the universe. It is easier to explain how a very large neural net can process data, or how nerves transmit signals, or how this or that behaviour should evolve, or why being able to build a mental model of other creatures is efficacious for hunting or mating with them and how that model could then be turned inwards (eureka!). It is easier to consider that the brain is a computer and that is how it can achieve this or that. But computers do not have subjective experience. All of this is answering Chalmer's Easy problem of how a creature, including human creatures, can behave as it does. A complete solution to the Easy problem would explain everything about other people except for the one important thing. That when they say they feel something, a real feeling is being had, and nothing else in the universe matters.

Some assumptions on the nature of subjective experience

Panpsychism

The world sags under the weight of articles on consciousness: on global workspaces, on integrated information theory, on high-order theories. How to find arrangements from which subjective experience can naturally emerge, like stepping back from a pointilist painting and seeing the wood for the trees. Sophisticated theories assume that consciousness is sophisticated, existing at the top of a hierarchy of self-reflection; others would give a thermostat a scintilla of experience. Panpsychism, which is more of an idea than a theory, is a belief about the level at which feeling enters the picture. It is in every brushstoke, not just the whole; feeling is literally in each fleck of paint or stone or atom. In Materialism we wondered whether consciousness is a strongly emergent phenomenon. If it is not, one might assume that at one lower level there is a simpler form of consciousness, and at the next level simpler again, in such a way that subjective experience does not have to appear all at once. To argue against strong emergence is to argue that consciousness is there all along.

Continuities of consciousness

The goal is an understanding of the objectively discontinuous nature of subjective experience. We identify three types of discontinuity: of time, life, and of matter. Of the three, the discontinuity of matter is the most difficult to accept, and for which we offer a couple of thought experiments (Transported to Mars). We introduce the terminology of a "subjective line" to explain what might be continuous to a person while broken to any observer of that person. A major difficulty is the distinction between a gradual turnover or a quick one, understood through Theseus' ship. We present various arguments (including transporter arguments) that if there is such a thing as a subjective line at all, it must be preserved despite the discontinuities of time, life, and matter. (That if is important.) Our initial definition of subjective immortality is that every subjective line is infinitely long. "Continuity" is an interesting word to use, as in the mathematical sense it means unbroken. That is not how it feels to be conscious at all. QUESTION: Should this be the first chapter?

Time disappeared

I had surgery on my shoulder to repair an old injury. Twenty years earlier, on a very English summer's day, I took a tumble into one of the fosses of Maiden Castle, an Iron Age hillfort. Picture a large flat hill protected by rings of deep ditches. The future Roman emperor Vespasian and his Second Legion ploughed through the warrior aristocracy of the Durotriges there. In our time it is used for grazing, and I remember after that alarming fall looking up at a line of sheep, no more concerned at my plight than the flock of little fluffy clouds above them. I like to think this is a more picturesque than average reason to require shoulder stabilization. The surgery took place at a clinic in San Francisco. I waited anxiously, heart rate too high, as the anesthesiologist began the-- it was over. I was sitting in a different room, no sensation on my righthand side, being told by the nurse that the surgery was over and I was ready to be taken home. Are you sure? I said. Several hours had disappeared as if excised by a scalpel.

We exist in time, which can speed and slow and skip. We know this already: a person will experience many more 4 pms than 4 ams. Sleep is not utterly disorientating because we usually wake in the same room as we fall asleep, and dreams give the impression that we are travelling continuously through time. Yet we dream for a quarter or less of the time spent asleep, the rest is a sort of oblivion. A person's subjective experience is, objectively, a stuttering thing, and there is in principle no limit to the amount of time that could be lost between stutters. What would the subjective experience be of a person who is frozen now, and somehow unfrozen in a thousand years?

The difference between a person's continuous subjective experience and the many objective discontinuities of that experience is obvious, and profound. A concept we will use to sharpen this distinction is the subjective line: the complete sequence of felt experiences a person has: waking experiences, dreams, every moment in which there is a there there for the person. Each moment on the subjective line is also a moment in which the person is alive, yet the reverse is not true. The subjective line does not include dreamless sleep, or the oblivion of anaesthetic, or (currently fanciful) periods in the deep freeze. When we are, the subjective line is. What makes the universe special is that for certain arrangements of matter, at certain times, an experience exists.

Imagine a lightbulb, shining bright, then dark, then bright again, blinking on and off. If we snip away all the moments in which the lightbulb is off, we have something equivalent to its subjective line. And in that view the lightbulb is always on until the moment it turns off forever. We are alive whether conscious or not, an unceasing chemistry; but we only really exist in time when having some subjective experience. In moments off the subjective line, we have to assume that something exists: a body, tens of trillions of cells, a hive of activity, a host of feedback loops, a world of chemistry. You, though, the you that operates just behind the eyes, you only exist on the subjective line.

This is not a new idea. Recall David Hume from Three quotations to set the scene, "So long am I insensible of myself, and may truly be said not to exist." Hume proceeds to describe a person as a bundle of perceptions, an idea we will pursue in more depth in Chapter 5. For now, our direction is towards an initial, naïve conception of subjective immortality: that every person's subjective line is infinitely long.

The three discontinuities

The first and easiest discontinuity is time. It is easy to believe because it is necessary to believe: that the person who falls asleep is the same person who wakes up. It is our lived experience. In fact, it takes a positive effort to not believe that this is true. Perhaps each morning a new person awakes who merely thinks that they are the continuation of yesterday's person? Perhaps after each conscious moment a new person exists, perhaps every subjective line is a single point? Another way of expressing the same thought: you have this moment, will you have another? Dualistic theories do not have this problem. If there is some separate, unchanging kernel of a person, a soul coincident with yet separate from the changing body, then of course that survives through time. In this book we stick to the materialist view, only matter and its arrangements, so there is no unchanging kernel we can point to. A person is in Heraclitan flux. For now though, let us continue to believe that stretches of time can routinely disappear from our lived experience, and yet we remain ourselves, our subjective line hiding every break.

The second discontinuity is life. Medicine has reached the point where it is almost a commonplace for a person to be clinically dead for a time, then be revived. It is generally accepted that the person who is alive again is the same person who was alive before. (Here it is dualistic theories that have trouble. Does the soul decide to return, or is it forced to?) We can stretch this intuition: think of the person frozen for a thousand years, what is it like for them? Most would agree that their experience would be being alive in the present, and then being alive a thousand years in the future, rather that: being alive in the present, followed by oblivion, then some other person appearing a thousand years later. Should the same be true if the process of freezing had the effect of destroying a person's old memories? I.e. if the revived person does not know that they are on to their second life? We are brushing against a strain of thought that goes back to John Locke and his Memory Theory of personal identity (from the Essay Concerning Human Understanding). Think, though, of the moment-to-moment experience of a person. Why should it matter what they remember? Memory is partial, fallible, constantly renewed and in being renewed, changed. What might count for legal definitions of identity is not interesting. Does a person's subjective line, their chain of experiences, continue if revived from death, and with no arbitrary time limit on when that revival must happen? It seems reasonable to say yes, it does.

The third discontinuity is matter. We are made of atoms, not always the same atoms. While it is a huge oversimplificiation to say that cellular replication means that our bodies are completely replaced every seven years, there is some truth to it. Skin cells are fully replaced every couple of years (welcome, dust); skeletal cells take a decade. Brain cells, though, are not replaced. Are they the unchanging core that gives personal continuity? If so, we have to stop here, and admit that matter is not the third discontinity, that the subjective line is broken by changes to the matter of which at least our brains are composed. The task of the following sections is to provide plausible arguments why even without brain cells being turned over as quickly as the rest of the body, they do change enough to conclude that the subjective line survives larger changes in matter.

Transported to Mars

The transporters of 1960s Star Trek are a marvellous story-telling technology invented as a cost-saving measure. A shimmering disappearance and reappearance is easier than animating a shuttle landing. Coincidentally, and for similarly contingent reasons, Dr Who's concept of regeneration was invented the following year. Both are useful ideas for clarifying the meaning of personal identity and continuity. The idea of a transporter was famously (within certain circles) used by Derek Parfit in his Reasons and Persons to tease out what we really mean by a person existing through time. Our interest here is in its use for justifying that the subjective line can survive changes in matter, for which we provide a new version of the thought experiment.

Consider a person on Earth. They wish to get to Mars (who can say why? It is an awful place, nevertheless), and have two options for doing so. One is to take a berth on in a spaceship, the other is to be transported directly there: to be scanned, turned to a beam of information, then reconstituted on Mars. Imagine that as a way of lessening the disorientation of suddenly appearing 200 million kilometres away, the transporter company offers an option whereby your new brain is recreated with additional memories of having taken a spaceship, the jump from Earth to Mars made smooth in retrospect. In this way, whether the person chooses to take a spaceship or the transporter, whether they choose to be bodily carried to Mars, or be destructively encoded into signals that are captured on Mars and used to recreate him, they would arrive on Mars in the same state. In fact we can push this a bit and say that in both cases, spaceship or transporter, the resulting person on Mars is identical, there is no physical difference between each option.

There is a difference of course. While in the spaceship option, the person is made of largely the same atoms on arriving on Mars as they were on Earth, in the transporter option, every atom has changed. It is not their matter which has travelled to Mars, it is a complete description of how to make them from matter. Our assumption is that the transporter is so faithful that there is no physical way to tell the transported version from the non-transported one. (We are verging on the "one-electron universe" idea of John Wheeler. That the reason why all electrons appear to be identical is that they are the same electron zipping forwards in time as an electron, then backwards as a positron. But we do not have to go that far, it should suffice for now to say: physically indistinguishable, rather than identical.)

We have two situations: in the first, a person has been taken, bodily, to Mars. It is easy to believe that their subjective line is unbroken. What, really, is the difference between being on a spaceship and sitting at home? If you are still you tomorrow, then you will still be you on Mars, albeit colder, and weighing less. In the second situation this is much less clear. The person will certainly believe that they are the continuation of that person on Earth, they will have a psychological connection; anyone who meets them would attest that yes, this is the person they remember. But they were destroyed. The person on Mars has not a single atom in common with the person on Earth. (We will save considerations like, what happens if two copies were made on Mars, for later.) What do things look like from the perspective of the subjective line?

Either the subjective line stops at the transporter and a new subjective line is created on Mars; or the same subjective line continues on Mars. Either the true subjective experience of the person is: life on Earth, then oblivion. Or life on Earth, then life on Mars. Imagine now that the first case is true. The subjective line is broken by a complete change in matter, to be transported is to be killed, and the newly reconstituted person is, genuinely, a new consciousness. What can we say about that?

We can say that the two versions of the person (spaceship and transporter) are physically identical (since that is what the transporter has done, we can safely assume this), but also differ in a single respect: one is the continuation of the subjective experience of a person on Earth, the other is not. The question is: where is this difference kept? We are religiously sticking to a materialist, physicalist viewpoint, remember. If there is a difference, it must be a physical difference. We have already ruled that out though. To underline what we have done here: by assuming that there is no supernatural non-physical component to consciousness, we have to conclude that transportation, the wholesale change of matter in which a person is composed, does not break the subjective line. Matter is our third discontinuity.

An objection: we have said that the two versions of the person are physically identical, yet are made from different atoms. What if consciousness is a hidden property of matter? What if two electrons, otherwise identical in every respect, nevertheless have a hidden quantity, one that somehow involves consciousness? We must admit that this is a powerful objection. One counter-argument is to consider that it implies that consciousness is both real and has no effect. If an atom can differ in whatever scintilla of consciousness it has, and yet that diffence cannot be told by any physical means, then the conscious portion does not have a physical effect on the world. Or, to be exact, which consciousness has no effect. This is not a very materialistic line of argument, and we have already pledged ourselves to materialism. Another counter-argument: consciousness seems to be a property that emerges from very large collections of atoms, and only at times. The difference between a living and a dead brain, a conscious or an unconscious one, is more about the motions of matter than its mere presence. It appears to be a macro property that emerges from the physical interactions of underlying particles. In which case, it is the interactions that count, i.e. the physical properties of that substrate, and not some further hidden facts within them.

An objection: while the two versions of the person do not differ, the worlds in which they exist certainly do. In one case a spaceship flew from Earth to Mars, in the other it did not. If a person is extended to encompass the world, then we cannot say they are the same, and our argument collapses. To make this clearer: we have argued that since persons A (spaceship) and B (transporter) are, after their respective travels, physically identical, we can conclude that their conscious selves are identical too. But how tight a circle do we draw around each version of this person? If we widen the circle a little, they can no longer be identical, as the worlds in which they each exist must not be the same. A brief tangent on philosphical zombies

An idea most associated with David Chalmers, who also coined the Hard problem of consciousness (of which, more soon), is the philosphical zombie. Imagine a person who has all the normal reactions of someone who is conscious, right down to every known neural correlate of consciousness (also, more later). And imagine that this person is not conscious, that there is no there for him, no actual feeling being had, just the appearance of it. Is that easy to imagine? If so, it suggests that there is something extra-physical about consciousness, something supernatural. In this book, we assume and will continue to assume physicalism, so what does this mean for the philosphical zombie? It must be impossible. It implies that there could exist two people, identical in every physical way, yet one is conscious, the other is not. But, as with our people sent by spaceship or transporter, if there is a real difference, there must be a physical difference to capture it.

To explain by analogy, two identical books must tell the same story. If one is a meditation on love and memory, while the other is a book of vegan recipes, some of the words really do have to differ. An interesting thought: in what way might two identical books tell different stories? If they are read by different people. Subjective experience is intrinsic though, it does not depend on an external observer, it is no one's interpretation but the experience itself.

The philosphical zombie thought experiment gives an intuition that consciousness must be something extra-physical, a feeling that it ought to be possible. Already today we can construct a robot with a realistic human face, who answers questions in a natural way. Full androids loiter around the corner. But plastic and metal and data centre half a state away are not physically anything like a human being. The thought experiment asks us to picture the external and imagine that the internal fits.

What would it look like if the subjective line was very short

When Wittgenstein was told it was rather obvious why people believed that the sun went around the Earth, because it looks like it does, he replied: "What would it have looked like if it had looked as though the Earth was rotating?" Presumably, exactly the same. (Or there is a joke to be had in which to look as though the Earth was the one that moved, it would have to be stationary.) We do not arrive on this Earth a blank piece of paper, with complete objectivity. We are already a book, filled with assumptions, ones that are easier or more difficult to overwrite. To try and understand a thing is to fit that thing into your understanding. It is when that thing is too awkward or strange to possibly fit that you are forced to cut or sand or grow your understanding. The Earth moves, most of us can accept that. A human is composed of trillions of cells, more or less working in concert. Subjective experience is real, senses coming together moment-by-moment as a perspective on the world. It is not a fiction, in the sense that the great mystery of how matter can feel could be dismissed by saying, it doesn't really; it does. This moment of experience exists (it is all we can know exists), what of the next one? It is easy to believe, as we have already said, that the subjective line lasts the length of a life. What would it feel like if it did not, if only a moment of experience is had then oblivion, because by the next moment the body has changed? What would it feel like if the subjective line is never more than a moment long. The body sails on, changing, generating more experiences, a constellation of cells and only mostly an individual. It would feel exactly the same. We only ever feel the current feeling.

As another way of approaching the same idea, consider a future pleasure or pain. Will you feel it? If you are your body then yes; yet are you your body, is that what it feels like? Does it not feel like your body is a carrier, a vessel, for you? A hand to draw your subjective line.

A thought experiment: the repeated experience. Imagine eating a strawberry. Now imagine that through some future wonder an exact copy of you eats a strawberry in exactly the same way. No brain imaging or experiment could tell the two experiences apart, every (physical) aspect is repeated. What would that feel like to you? Would it feel like eating a strawberry only once, or would it feel like eating a strawberry once, and then (with no memory of having had the experience before) of eating a strawberry again? What is the difference, why would it not be you having that extra strawberry, if you are your body, and the body is the same?

One difficulty here is a stubborn slide back to dualism, to the body-as-vessel. There is a good reason why it is so natural or even necessary a belief: intelligence is for planning, it exists to control the future, to keep a collection of cells viable in a way that maximises its replication. A self is created that believes that it, like the body, will exist in the future, that the choices it makes matter for itself, that the body is in service of it, and not the other way around. This would look and feel exactly the same as the situation in which a self is generated whenever it is necessary or convenient, then destroyed.

Unconscious metamorphoses (the evil zookeeper)

We are groping towards something fundamental in our thoughts of a self. What do we mean when we say, a person? Is a person that which is conscious, the subjective experience of a body (with all the discontinuities we discuss above); or is a person the body itself, moving smoothly through time? We can start to tease out our real meaning with a thought experiment.

Imagine that tonight, after falling asleep, you are taken by an evil zookeeper, drugged to oblivion. The zookeeper then performs a lengthly surgery on your body and brain at the cellular level, splitting here, moving there, rewiring your mind (and repurposing unneeded neurons as other cells), taking great care that at every step you remain alive, until, at last, what the evil zookeeper has created is a living tiger, of exactly your previous mass, composed of exactly your cells. The tiger wakes, and looks around. What would your subjective experience be? Would it be falling asleep, and then waking as a tiger? Or would it be to fall asleep, and never wake?

Now imagine a very evil zookeeper, who, rather than carefully shifting your cells, starts by putting you through a blender, rendering you down to atoms, to the carbon and oxygen and nitrogen and... from which he then proceeds to construct a tiger. The tiger then wakes.

Finally, imagine a lazy zookeeper, who does not go through all the fantastical trouble of manufacturing a tiger. No, he merely feeds your body to a tiger cub over the course of a week. The tiger cub, whose atoms are your atoms, wakes up. Do you wake up then, too?

In the third example, it feels obvious that you have simply died. (Would the subjective experience of a farmed chicken be to exist, fattened quickly amidst its thousands of cousins, and then be a well-fed human, going about his day?) In the second, probably so. In the first, what do we think?

Transported to Mars times two (and divided by two)

What if two copies were made? What would the subjective experience be of a person on Earth, who is destructively scanned by a transporter, beamed to Mars, and then reconstructed as two identical persons? Would they have a manner of dual consciousness? Imagine that they are reconstructed on Mars in different rooms, one painted red, the other painted blue. Would the subjective experience of the person be: to exist on Earth, to step inside the transporter, and then to appear in a red room, or in a blue room?

One option we should discount is that their experience is somehow of being in both red and blue rooms. Experience comes through the senses, and there are two quite separate sets of senses involved, one which only knows of a red room, the other, of a blue room. There is no purple option. But then, which is it? We cannot conclude that it is red without equally concluding blue. We are left with the idea that a subjective line can branch. What would it feel like to the person? Of being in a red room, and only knowing that. And also of being in a blue room, and only knowing that. We are approaching something, a way to get beyond a linear, intuitive, sane (one might say) idea of subjective experience, one tied to a particular body and the single path that body takes.

Our person has become two people, each with their own subjective experience, and both being the continuation of that person's subjective line. This is awkward and so perhaps the transporter has some protections in place against it. In the event that two identical people are created, the transporter re-scans both, creating two identical corpuses of data, which it then deduplicates (since data are data), and sends back to Earth as a single beam, reconstituted as a single person again. One subjective line became two, and now converges to one again.

We could ask: so what does this single person remember, once back on Earth, a red room or a blue room? That would be a mistake. At the point that one copy is standing in a red room and the other a blue room, they are no longer identical: to perceive is to change. Their re-integration would only work if, say, they had their eyes closed; or if some judicious memory surgery were done so both believed they had been in a purple room. In this way, joining subjective lines does not seem to have the same conceptual problem as splitting them does, of the difficulty of imagining what it could possibly feel like. When the line splits into the future, you have to ask: which future will I be in? When two lines re-join, there is no choice, no question of what is being experienced as there is only one experience to be had.

The Vertiginous Question

I remember puzzling as a child over the problem of why I was me and not someone else. At times I was afraid that by looking at someone I would somehow swap with them, and then not know how to behave. Later, not immediately, I realised that I would know how to behave as them, as I would be them, with whatever skills and experience they had. (Perhaps part of my concern was the reflected worry that I did not know, myself, how to behave. I did not want to become someone who knew themselves even less -- imagine.) Later still, I concluded that the problem was a non-problem. I was me because there existed a being with certain properties, who had his own thoughts, and I was the thoughts. I was that which I was, as someone else was that which he was. It was not that I could not swap with another person, so much as there would not be a feeling associated with the swap. I could be everyone, feel everything, and never experience that fact, never know the totality because all experiences are just one experience at a time. These thoughts from decades ago have some relevance for whether the subjective line, personal continuity, exists.

And of course the problem has a name, albeit one coined years after the child me worried away at it: the Vertiginous Question. Say there are two people in the world, both alive and conscious. One sits in a red room, and the other sits in a blue room. One says: "I am experiencing red." The other says: "I am experiencing blue."

"Why," the first asks, "am I experiencing red?" Because you are in a red room. "But why could I not be the person sitting in a blue room?" Because a person is in a red room, their brain is processing that fact, there is an experience associated with that. "So," the person in the red room says, "you are saying that I am the person in a red room because I am experiencing the colour red? Why do you think that answers my question?"

It is the same confusion: the question is a real one if there is some way of separating the seat of experience, the subjective view, the feeler of the feeling, the experiencer of the experience; of separating that from the physical system underlying it. There is a red room, a person, a brain, a self experiencing the colour red. It is too easy to think of that last part, the experiencer as being moveable. That it is somehow arbitrary that that self happens to be associated with the body in the red room and not the one in the blue room. But there is no separate entity, there is just the physical, atoms in flux.
