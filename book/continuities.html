Continuities of consciousness

The goal is an understanding of the objectively discontinuous nature of subjective experience. We identify three types of discontinuity: of time, life, and of matter. Of the three, the discontinuity of matter is the most difficult to accept. We introduce the terminology of a "subjective line" to explain what might be continuous to a perso while broken to any observer of that person. We present various arguments (including transporter arguments) that if there is such a thing as a subjective line at all, it must be preserved despite the discontinuities of time, life, and matter. (That if is important.) Our initial definition of subjective immortality is that every subjective line is infinitely long. "Continuity" is an interesting word to use, as in the mathematical sense it means unbroken. That is not how it feels to be conscious at all.

Time disappeared

Not too long ago I had surgery on my shoulder to repair an old injury. Twenty years earlier, on a very English summer's day, I took a tumble down the side of the Iron Age hill fort known as Maiden Castle. Arms flailing, sheep watching with no more concern than the little fluffy clouds I watched, on my back at the bottom of the fosse, once I had come to a painful stop. I like to think this is a more picturesque than average reason to require shoulder stabilization. The surgery took place at a clinic in San Francisco, and I remember waiting anxiously, heart rate too high, as the anesthesiologist began the-- it was over. I was sitting in a different room, no sensation on my righthand side, being told my the nurse that the surgery had been a success. Are you sure it's already happened? I said. Several hours had disappeared as if excised by a scalpel.

We exist in time, which can speed and slow and skip. You know this already: you will have experienced many more 4pms than 4ams.
